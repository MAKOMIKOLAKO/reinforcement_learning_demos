CartPole · REINFORCE

A REINFORCE agent learns a stochastic policy directly from full-episode returns — no value function, no critic. Every step it samples an action from a two-way probability distribution output by a small neural net; it never just picks the "best" move. Scrub the slider below to watch the policy go from random flailing to a steady balance as training progresses.

Environment

State space

The standard Gym environment exposes a 4-dimensional vector on every step:

  • cart_position — the cart's horizontal position on the track.
  • cart_velocity — the cart's horizontal velocity.
  • pole_angle — the pole's angle from vertical upright, in radians.
  • pole_angular_velocity — the rate of change of that angle.

Action space

Two discrete actions make up the entire : push the cart left, or push the cart right. Each is a fixed-magnitude force applied in one direction — there's no continuous control over how hard to push.

Reward function

The is simple: +1 for every timestep the pole stays upright. The ends — and reward stops — when any of three things happens: the pole angle exceeds about 12° (0.209 radians) from vertical, the cart leaves the track bounds (±2.4 units), or the episode reaches the 500-step cap for CartPole-v1.

REINFORCE vs. PPO

REINFORCE (this demo) updates its policy from the total return of a whole episode, with no learned value function to reduce variance — so training tends to look noisier and less stable, and progress across checkpoints can be uneven. The Pendulum demo on this site uses PPO, an actor-critic method that also learns a value function (a "critic") to judge states, giving it a steadier, lower-variance training signal and much smoother improvement across checkpoints.

How REINFORCE learns from a trajectory

Two things worth noticing here. First, there is no value network anywhere in this diagram — that's deliberate, not an oversight. REINFORCE has nothing playing the role of a or critic. Second, the whole episode's has to be collected in the Trajectory Buffer before any learning update happens. That's different from algorithms that learn from individual transitions step by step (like DQN's replay buffer sampling) — REINFORCE has to wait until the episode ends before it knows the and can compute a gradient.

Why REINFORCE, and its tradeoffs

Policy-based, no baseline

REINFORCE is purely -based: the policy network directly outputs a probability distribution over actions, and the raw episode is used to weight the policy gradient. Nothing subtracts a learned to reduce the variance of that signal.

Simple and general, but high-variance

That simplicity is also REINFORCE's strength: because it only needs a distribution to sample from, it works for any action space — discrete (like CartPole's left/right) or continuous — with almost no extra machinery. The cost is variance: two trajectories of identical underlying policy quality can end up with very different returns just from stochastic bad luck during sampling, so training tends to look noisier and less monotonic than an algorithm with a baseline. Compare the checkpoint progression on this page — scrub the slider above and watch the average-episode-length chart — against the smoother progression on the Pendulum/PPO page, where a learned value function damps that noise out.

On-policy: no replay buffer reuse

REINFORCE is strictly : every trajectory used for an update must come from the current policy. Once the policy changes, older trajectories can't be reused for further learning. Contrast that with DQN, which is off-policy and reuses old transitions from a over and over. That reuse gap hurts REINFORCE's — it needs more fresh environment interaction to make the same amount of learning progress.
