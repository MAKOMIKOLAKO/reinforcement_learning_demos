DQN vs. REINFORCE vs. PPO

All three demos on this site are solving the same kind of problem — an agent interacting with an MDP and trying to maximize reward — but they get there in genuinely different ways. This page lines them up side by side so the differences are concrete, not just three different acronyms.

Aspect DQN value-only REINFORCE policy-only PPO actor-critic
Learns a value function? Yes — a Q-network estimating the value of each action No Yes — a critic estimating state value, used to reduce variance
Learns a policy directly? No — behavior falls out of argmax over Q-values Yes — a network outputs action probabilities directly Yes — an actor network outputs action probabilities directly
On-policy or off-policy? Off-policy — learns from a replay buffer of old experience On-policy — every update needs fresh trajectories from the current policy On-policy (with limited reuse via clipping)
Typical sample efficiency High — every transition gets reused many times Low — each trajectory is used once, then discarded Medium — a handful of epochs per batch of trajectories
Typical training stability Medium — can diverge without target networks / careful tuning Low — high-variance gradient estimates, noisy learning curves High — the critic and clipped objective damp destructive updates
Works with continuous action spaces? No — needs a discrete action set to take an argmax over Yes, with a suitable output distribution (e.g. Gaussian) Yes — the demo here outputs a continuous torque
Real-world use-case hint Recommender systems / discrete decision-making with logged historical data to replay Small, well-understood discrete-action problems where simplicity beats speed Robotics and continuous control where stable, reliable convergence matters

When you'd actually reach for each one

DQN

Reach for DQN when the action space is discrete and you can afford to store and revisit past experience. Its replay buffer is what makes it so sample-efficient — every transition the agent ever saw can be replayed many times during training, instead of being thrown away after one gradient step. The tradeoff is that this whole approach is built around picking the best of a fixed, enumerable set of actions: there's no clean way to take an argmax over a continuous range of torques or velocities, so DQN doesn't extend to continuous control without substantial reworking (e.g. discretizing the action space, which throws away precision).

REINFORCE

Reach for REINFORCE when you want the simplest possible policy gradient method, or the problem is small enough that sample efficiency doesn't matter much. It optimizes the policy directly by pushing up the probability of actions that led to high reward, which is general enough to work in discrete or continuous action spaces alike with no value function required. The cost of that simplicity is variance: because it relies on full-episode returns as its only signal, gradient estimates are noisy, so training tends to need many more episodes and a lot of patience compared to methods that bootstrap off a learned value estimate.

PPO

Reach for PPO when you need continuous actions and stable, repeatable training — which is why it's a default choice for robotics and other continuous-control tasks. Its critic gives updates a lower-variance baseline than REINFORCE's raw returns, and its clipped objective keeps any single policy update from moving too far, so training rarely blows up the way plain policy gradients can. That stability isn't free: PPO is more moving parts than REINFORCE (an actor and a critic, plus a clipping hyperparameter to tune) and, like REINFORCE, it's still on-policy, so it can't reuse old experience the way DQN's replay buffer can.
