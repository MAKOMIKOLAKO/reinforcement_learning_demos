Pendulum · PPO (actor-critic)

A Proximal Policy Optimization agent controlling a frictionless inverted pendulum. The network outputs a continuous torque, applied every physics step to swing the pendulum up and hold it balanced. Scrub the training-progress slider to compare an untrained (random-torque) checkpoint against the fully trained policy.

Environment

Pendulum-v1 is a continuous-control task: instead of choosing among a handful of discrete moves, the agent outputs a real-valued torque, and every reward it gets is a penalty that only ever pushes toward zero. Here's the full picture, spelled out.

State space

A 3-dimensional vector: [cos(θ), sin(θ), angular velocity], where θ is the pendulum's angle away from upright.

The angle is encoded as its cosine and sine rather than as the raw angle θ itself. A raw angle wraps discontinuously from +π to -π as the pendulum swings through the bottom — the same physical position would otherwise be represented by two numbers at opposite ends of the range. The cos/sin pair moves smoothly around the unit circle instead, so nearby physical angles always map to nearby numbers, with no seam for a to trip over.

Action space

Continuous, not discrete: a single torque value in [-2.0, 2.0] N·m. There's no menu of moves to pick from — the outputs a real number (or a distribution over real numbers) directly in that range, which is a genuinely different shape of problem than Snake's four discrete directions or CartPole's two-way push.

Reward function

Exactly -(θ_norm² + 0.1×velocity² + 0.001×action²), where θ_norm is the angle from upright normalized to [-π, π].

Every term in that sum is squared and negated, so every single reward is negative or, at best, exactly zero — never positive. There is no "good" reward to chase, only a penalty to shrink. The objective is to minimize the magnitude of that penalty: get the angle near 0 (upright), keep angular velocity low (stay still), and spend as little torque as possible.

Worth noticing when comparing pages: Snake and CartPole are both set up as positive-reward-seeking tasks (score points, survive longer, both give reward you want more of). Pendulum flips that framing — it's a pure penalty-minimization task where the best possible episode still only ever accumulates non-positive reward.

How this differs from CartPole

PPO learns two things at once: a policy (the actor, which picks the torque you see applied here) and a value function (the critic, which estimates how good a state is). Only the actor is exported to these checkpoints — the critic never runs at inference time, here or in the real world.

What the critic buys you is training stability: it gives PPO a low-variance baseline to judge actions against, whereas REINFORCE (the CartPole demo on this site) learns from raw episode returns alone. In practice that means this checkpoint slider should sweep from flailing to competent swing-up-and-balance more smoothly and consistently than CartPole's REINFORCE progression, which tends to be noisier checkpoint-to-checkpoint.

Physics

Reimplements Gym's Pendulum-v1 exactly: gravity 10.0, mass 1.0, length 1.0, 0.05s timestep, torque clipped to ±2.0 N·m, angular velocity clipped to ±8.0 rad/s. Episodes run 200 steps and then auto-restart from a random initial angle and angular velocity, matching Gym's reset distribution.

Why PPO, and its tradeoffs

REINFORCE (used on the CartPole page) updates its policy from raw episode alone — a noisy signal, since the same action can look great or terrible purely because of what randomly happened later in the episode. PPO adds a : the critic learns to predict how good a state already is, and that prediction gets subtracted from the observed return to compute an . Subtracting a state-dependent baseline doesn't change what the optimal policy is, but it does remove a large chunk of the variance from the gradient signal — the policy update reacts to "was this action better or worse than expected here," not to the raw, noisy total.

The other piece is the . In plain terms: it caps how far a single update is allowed to shift the policy's action probabilities. Without that cap, one unlucky batch of rollout data could yank the policy sharply in the wrong direction, and because training is — the next batch of data comes from whatever policy that bad update just produced — a big enough mistake can spiral rather than get corrected. The clip keeps each update small enough that a bad batch does limited damage. It's worth actually comparing the two checkpoint sliders side by side: this page's progression from flailing torque to a held balance should look smoother and more consistent checkpoint-to-checkpoint than CartPole's noisier REINFORCE progression.

None of this is free. PPO trains two networks instead of one (actor and critic), and needs extra machinery REINFORCE doesn't: advantage estimation, a value-function loss for the critic, and a clip-ratio hyperparameter to tune. That's meaningfully more implementation complexity in exchange for the lower-variance, more stable training you can see in the checkpoint progression above.
